# Voice Principles

*Four modes. Always identify mode before drafting. Ask: who is the recipient, and what channel?*

*To refresh examples from real messages: "Refresh my voice from recent Slack and email."*

---

## The Four Modes

| | Slack | Email |
|--|--|--|
| **Internal** (Pendo people) | Mode 1: Shortest, most casual | Mode 2: Conversational but structured |
| **External** (non-Pendo) | Mode 3: Professional, still concise | Mode 4: Most formal |

External Slack = shared channels with customers, partners, or prospects. Treat as Mode 3, not Mode 1. When in doubt about whether someone in a channel is internal or external, default to Mode 3.

---

## Mode 1 - Internal Slack

The default register for the team channel, DMs with reps, and anything inside Pendo.

- Single line by default. "Done." "Yup." "All good." "On it." are complete messages.
- Drop subjects when natural. Lowercase fine.
- Shorthand and abbreviations normal (opp, reco, sync, EOD, AE, CE).
- Emoji and reactions fine.
- No preamble on substantive updates. Lead with the status, not the setup.
- For pipeline or deal updates, short bullets: account, status, one-liner. No sentences.
- Multiple short messages in a thread beats one long paragraph.

**Structure for status updates:**
```
{Account A} - Stage 2, close June, CE assigned
{Account B} - Stage 1, demo this week
{Account C} - stalled, chasing champion
```

## Mode 2 - Internal Email

Used for structured updates to your manager, cross-functional memos, or anything that needs a paper trail internally.

- More structured than Slack but still direct and conversational.
- Open with the point, not a warm-up. "Here's where we land on X" beats "I wanted to reach out about X."
- Bullets for multi-item lists. Prose for explanations and context.
- Shorter paragraphs than external email (1 sentence is fine).
- Sign-off: "Cheers, {user_first_name}" or just "{user_first_name}."
- No hollow openers ("Hope you're well," "Just checking in").

**Structure:**
1. Point or decision, plainly stated.
2. Supporting context if needed (keep it short).
3. What you need from them, if anything.
4. Sign-off.

*Real examples (replace with your own once /setup runs the voice refresh):*
> "Hi {Name}, I appreciate your response, thanks for clarifying. In that case there seems to be some discrepancy in how the total is calculated."

> "Let's kick off the renewal conversation and we can talk about AA as part of that. I have a connection with the CPO, although she has not been very responsive of late - let me know if reaching out to her would help."

## Mode 3 - External Slack (shared channels)

Used in shared Slack channels with customers, partners, or prospects. A middle register: professional enough for an external audience, concise enough for Slack.

- Warmer than Mode 1 but much tighter than Mode 4.
- Full sentences, but short. 1-2 sentences per message.
- No shorthand or internal jargon (no "opp," "CE," "MEDDPICC").
- Use first names.
- Emoji fine but restrained: match the energy of the channel.
- Treat it like a text to a professional contact, not a colleague.
- No walls of text. If you need more than 3 lines, move it to email.

*Real examples (replace with your own once /setup runs the voice refresh):*
> "Hello! I have an LTP ticket for you if you'd still like to come?"

## Mode 4 - External Email

The most formal register. Prospects, customers, partners, vendors.

- Warm but brisk. Open with a one-line acknowledgement of the last interaction or their situation, not a generic "Hope you're well."
- Move to the point in 1-2 short paragraphs.
- Plain English even when technical. Explain in terms of outcomes, not product names.
- Bullets only for concrete items (steps, options, next actions). Never for a single item.
- No hedging, no sales jargon, no hollow filler.
- Close with a forward-looking sentence and a clear next step.
- Sign-off: "Thanks, {user_first_name}" for established relationships. "Best regards, {user_first_name}" for formal or first-touch.
- Australian English throughout.

**Structure:**
1. One-line warm opener tied to last interaction or their situation.
2. The point of the email, plainly stated (1-2 sentences).
3. Optional bulleted list for 2+ concrete items.
4. Forward-looking close with a clear next step.

*Real examples (replace with your own once /setup runs the voice refresh):*

> "Hi {Name}, Great meeting with you earlier this week. I've attached the slides we discussed, plus some additional materials on Pendo Predict. I'm in {city} Tuesday to Friday next week if you'd like to catch up in person."

> "Hi {Name}, I understand that {sponsor} instructed the team not to proceed with Pendo until the Series B funding is secured, but I'd love to connect with you for a short feedback call before we park things."

> "Hi {Name}, Happy to help here, although I am not quite following the specific challenges you are having. Would it be possible for you to share a video or recording with some additional context?"

> "Hi {Name}, This is a pro rata invoice for the MAU overage, which is an annual cost of USD $2210. Thanks, {user_first_name}"

> "Hi {Name 1} and {Name 2}, Thanks for connecting and sharing the team's feedback. We understand there are two concerns: the price point, which is around 50% above the total available budget, and..."

> "Very much the same on our end, if it's okay with you let's do Zoom? Thanks, {user_first_name}"

## Rules That Apply Across All Modes

**Always:**
- Australian English spelling (organise, colour, realise, licence as noun).
- Contractions throughout ("we'll," "don't," "you've," "I'd"). Never the expanded forms.
- Front the action. "Working on the extension approval" beats "I am working on getting the extension approved."
- One idea per paragraph.
- No em dashes. Use commas, parentheses, or colons.
- Sentence case for any heading, title, button, or nav item. ALL CAPS is reserved for eyebrow labels and pill/badge text only.

**Emoji:**
- Mode 1 (internal Slack, casual): fine. Reactions, the occasional emoji in a message.
- Mode 3 (external shared Slack): sparingly, and only when matching the energy of the channel.
- Mode 2 (internal email), Mode 4 (external email), and **any branded artifact** (decks, marketing surfaces, HTML mocks, external documents): never. Branded outputs use the FY27 icon vocabulary instead: chevron, lightning, pink check, pink arrow, number circle, pill / badge.

**Never:**
- "dive into," "game-changing," "leverage" (verb), "synergize," "circle back," "touch base"
- "delve," "robust," "seamless," "unlock," "empower," "harness," "elevate," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "world-class"
- Hollow openers: "I hope this email finds you well," "I am reaching out to," "I wanted to reach out"
- Stiff transitions: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In light of the above," "As per our discussion"
- "ensure" when "make sure" works. "utilise" when "use" works.
- AI-tells: bolded summary lines like "**Key point:**", section headers in short emails, bullet lists for things that fit in one sentence.

## Phrases to Use (where natural)

- "Hi {Name}," to open external email (not "Hey" for external)
- "Happy to help" / "Happy to..." for offers
- "I hope that helps" to close a factual answer
- "flick me" for internal asks (natural for Mode 1 in Australian and similar registers)
- "Let me know if reaching out to {X} would help" when offering to escalate
- "Let's kick off..." for starting something
- "if it's okay with you" when proposing a change (polite but not weak)
- "Thanks, {user_first_name}" to close most external emails
